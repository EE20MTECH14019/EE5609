\section{Problem}
\item Triangles on the same base (or equal bases) and having equal areas lie between the same parallels.
\section{Explanation}
\item Triangles $\triangle ABC$ and $\triangle ABD$ are two triangles lying on the same base AB such that area of $\triangle ABC$ is equal to area of $\triangle ABD$. Now we join $C$ and $D$ and and drop perpendiculars onto $AB$ from $C$ and $D$. Lets say the foot of the perpendiculars are $E$ and $F$.
\item As $CE \perp AB$ and $DF \perp AB$, we can write that
\begin{align}
    \label{eq1}
    (\vec{C}-\vec{E})^T(\vec{A}-\vec{B})=0\\
    \label{eq2}
    (\vec{D}-\vec{F})^T(\vec{A}-\vec{B})=0
\end{align}
\item From equations \eqref{eq1} and \eqref{eq2}
\begin{align}
    \label{eq3}
    (\vec{C}-\vec{E})=k(\vec{D}-\vec{F})
\end{align}
\item Equation \eqref{eq3} shows that $CE \parallel DF$. Also given that 
\begin{align}
    area(\triangle ABC)=area(\triangle ABD)\\
    \implies \frac{1}{2}(\vec{AB} \times \vec{CA})=\frac{1}{2}(\vec{AB} \times \vec{BD})
\end{align}
\item Using the triangle law of vectors,
\begin{align}
    \implies (\vec{AB} \times (\vec{CE}+\vec{EA}))=(\vec{AB} \times (\vec{DF}+\vec{FB}))\\
    \label{farea}
    \implies (\vec{AB} \times \vec{CE})=(\vec{AB} \times \vec{DF})
\end{align}
\item From equation \eqref{farea}, $CE$=$DF$. 
\item As $CE \parallel DF$ and $CE$=$DF$, we could say that CDEF forms a parallelogram. And $CD \parallel EF$ as $CD$ and $EF$ are opposite sides of the parallelogram CDEF. From this, we can conclude that $CD \parallel AB$.
\item Hence $\triangle ABC$ and $\triangle ABD$ lie between the same parallels AB and CD.
